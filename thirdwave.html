Third Wave Coffee


Sketch, Illustrator, Mobile

Overview


It's hard to find good coffee. I recently returned from a cross-country roadtrip, the goal of which was partially to explore great coffee across the U.S. Along the way, it became very apparent that there lacks a good product to discover the high quality coffee that is now nearly ubiquitous across the country. Yelp, Google Places, and Foursquare place an emphasis on things like service, food, vibe, and venue rather than on the coffee itself. This provides a good experience for many users, but leaves coffee lovers with little guidance or direction. There is a small but rapidly growing craft coffee community that has no good way to discover local roasters and share coffee experiences.

At the time, my solution consisted of a couple relatively convoluted methods. To discover must-try craft coffee in a particular city, I would cross reference online coffee writings with the intersection of Yelp’s results for “roaster” and “pour over.” Then, I had pore through reviews and images, seeking the roaster that a cafe served. I had take notes on a particular coffee bean on a note-taking app while keeping scores on a personally calibrated scale. I had seen similar problems solved well with apps like Untappd for beer, which has become very popular. I decided to create a simple, beautiful app for both coffee people and non-coffee people.

Third Wave will have 3 basic features:

  • Discover New Beans

  • Record Tasting Notes

  • Rate Coffee Beans

  • Future: Share Coffee Experiences


Challenge


  • Creating an app that is approachable and helpful to people of all knowledge levels

  • Immediately instill a sense of trust

  • A user should feel like they’re getting a recommendation from a friendly barista

Research


I've informally interviewed people about their coffee habits. Most people reacted positively to the concept of the app. A majority of them identified that they wanted a way to research the bean before they bought it- whether it would suit their tastes or if it was even good. Then, the more avid coffee drinkers wanted the ability to track what they drank and what they thought of the bean.


Identifying 3 common user questions

  • Have I had this bean before?
  • Did I like the coffee bean?
  • Do other people like this bean?


Brainstorm


I prioritized the search flow user exprience, because it is the core of entire application experience.

Some questions that came out from sketching (plus many more): what's really important for a user to know? what level of detail should we get into? how will the rating system work? how will we get the data to show ? People can brew a coffee in 10 different ways, and it can taste differently. How do you deal with that?





Content First


I mapped out the possible pieces of information that would be relevant. For each piece of data, I needed to answer the question of whether it would accomplish the goal of the app. The main concern was overwhelming users with data. Each piece of data would have an exponential effect. Another concern would be the effect on users who were newer to coffee. I didn't want to alienate or intimidate that demographic.

Next Steps


Since, Third Wave is still in its infancy, I am currently focusing on the high level user experience as a whole. Later phases will allow me to focus on micro‐interactions, which I plan on using Framer.js and Marvel for higher fidelity prototyping.

I have found prototyping to be the most effective way to gain meaningful feedback on design decisions. I will also need to figure out the strategy for data propogation.
