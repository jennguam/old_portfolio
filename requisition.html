Reinventing Job Requisition Forms


As job reqs continue to be a key component in the recruiting and hiring process, the need grows ever more increasing to make the process of creating one easier and less tedious.

My Role


As lead designer on this project, I was responsible for redesigning the flow, the visual design, as well as the interaction design.


Understand & Ideation

I partnered with a lead designer to uncover insights and translate user needs and stories into features.


Experience Strategy & Vision

I created layouts and prototypes to share the vision, design principles, and content strategy. This helped to cultivate ideas, gain alignment, and drive decisions.


Coordination, Team Work, and Communication

I collaborated with engineers to translate product features and better understand technological constraints.


Design Execution & Validation

I designed responsively for desktop, tablet, iOS, and Android devices. I executed journeys, wireframes, prototypes and design specs.



Stakeholders

I designed and presented works to gain buy‐in from executives, senior stakeholders, and large clients like GE and Cisco throughout the project lifecycle.






Problem


To redesign the requisition form that would be simple and easy to use


As a recruiter or HR administrator at a enterprise company, there are a lot of moving parts and people involved in recruiting. It can get really messy. A job requisition is central to the entire recruiting experience, which is why keeping information organized and fields easy to fill out is so important.


What's wrong?

  • Too many fields - Our existing requisition form has 21 fields with user confusion as to what exactly they all meant.
  • Ungrouped fields - Related fields were interspersed
  • Broken flow - A user would have to go to 3 different places on the platform just to finish creating a requisition
  • No guidance - It was unclear to the user what the next step was.
  • Unclear distinction - Users didn’t know which fields would be public and which were private. This caused trust issues because of the severe consequences of disclosing sensitive information like salary data to the public.

My Solution

  • To reduce the amount of fields
  • Group the fields into sections that had similar purposes to help the more easily form a mental model of how the data will be stored
  • Guide the user through clear steps instead of presenting it as one large step
  • Clarify what the purpose of each section as well as help the user understand the implications of their actions



Approach


Exploration and Research

There were too many fields. My first task was to understand what each and every field's purpose was and group them accordingly. I interviewed stakeholders and began dissecting. On a Google Doc, I outlined and defined each element. This way it is all laid out and I can easily organize them into buckets that made more sense.


Information Architecture



Placeholder Text


As part of our redesign, I also had to come up with a visual refresh of our new product. So, I spent around 30 minutes coming up with rough sketches of how to improve the way we present form elements.


One big problem with our existing forms is the usage of placeholders instead of labels. Yes, they look nice and they do save on vertical space. But once the user clicks on the text box, the label disappears and the user can't double check if they have written in the right field.


To make sure, the user will have to copy what's in the field, erase it, read the placeholder, and then paste it back for each field. Another concern is that when users see something already written inside a text box, they might assume it's already been prefilled and ignore it.




This new design solves a a lot of problems.

  • The label now very clearly indicates what the field is and what input is expected
  • I increased the height of the input field to 45px, so it would also be easily tappable on a mobile device.

Guide the user


Navigation Block

A navigation block describes the steps involved in the form. As the user progresses through the process, the navigation block is updated. The current step is highlighted, giving a clear indication to the user where they are and how much further there is to go. It also gives the the user context to easily move back and forth through the form in cases where they forgot a detail. This allows flexibility for the user to fill out all the fields or only the fields that they need for now.


This is critical to maintain the user's focus throughout the process of filling in data in to the system. Not only does this pattern provide users an overview of how far they have gone, but it also provides a visible end, which gives them a goal to aim for.







Grouping the fields with the same purpose


No one likes to fill out forms. Users should be able to complete forms quickly and without confusion. Our solution was to break the user input process into smaller, more manageable steps. Each step was broken out into logical blocks where the flow from one to the next became a story rather than a disconnected never-ending maze of fields. Grouping related fields together helps users make sense of the information they must fill in.


Instead of 21 fields and 10 tabs, I've now broken it down to 7 steps.
